Placebos (and periods) on the Pill—

What’s the point?

On birth control, having a period is totally unnecessary. Why don’t more people know?

By Rebecca Rand

If you’ve ever been on the pill, you’ve probably seen something like this: a pack with 21 days of “active” pills with pregnancy-preventing progestin, and 7 “placebos,” which contain…nothing.

Credit: Rebecca Rand

On day 22, when you switch from one to the other, the sudden drop in progestin triggers your uterine lining to shed. This resembles a period. But when you’re on birth control, doctors call it a “withdrawal bleed.”

Doctors have known for years that you don’t need to bleed every month on the pill. In fact, you don’t need to bleed ever. But when the pill was designed in the 50s, the inventors believed it was better to design the pill so that women would bleed every month. Such bleeding, they thought, would serve a few purposes:1

  • To reassure women that they were not pregnant
  • To reassure women that they were not permanently infertile
  • To appease the Catholic Church, whom they hoped would approve a birth control pill that didn’t seem to disrupt a woman’s natural cycle.

It didn’t work out with the Catholic Church. But for over half a century, the pill has persisted in this cyclic format, with 20-ish days of pregnancy-preventing hormones, and several days of sugar pills, or placebos, in between. And surveys show that plenty of people on the pill are happy with this setup.2

But plenty still aren’t. And many still don’t know that bleeding on the pill is entirely optional.3 And for this reason, many myths about periods on the pill persist.

A recent survey4 of nearly 3,000 adolescents and young adults has found that a significant portion of young people who menstruate are wary of birth control methods that eliminate their periods, and many believe myths that the “placebo period” is necessary to preserve fertility and prevent harm to the body. Survey respondents from the South and those who came from Christian families were more likely to believe these myths than other participants.

Numerous studies have identified several common myths about birth control and menstruation across the globe:

Myth:

The pill was designed with a “period week” for medical reasons

Fact:

There is no physiological reason to bleed on the pill. Some folks find it comforting or reassuring. Others don't.

Myth:

It’s safer to bleed every month when you’re on the birth control pill.

Fact:

There is no significant difference in side effects or adverse outcomes between patients who take the pill without bleeding and patients who do bleed.5

Myth:

If you don’t bleed on the pill, the blood will build up inside you, causing bloating and pain.

Fact:

The hormones in the pill keep the menstrual tissue lining your uterus at a constant level. It doesn’t really grow when you’re on the pill, so there’s no risk of getting “backed up.”

Myth:

If you don’t menstruate on the pill, you have no way of knowing you’re not pregnant.

Fact:

While bleeding during the placebo week on the pill is a sign you aren’t pregnant, you also can also take a pregnancy test.

Myth:

Menstruation helps “clean you out”--and not menstruating leads to toxic buildup.6

Fact:

Menstrual blood is not toxic. Remember, the uterus is where a fetus can grow–it’s not full of poison.

The vagina already cleans itself with vaginal discharge throughout the month—it doesn’t need the extra help from menstrual blood.

Myth:

People who take the pill to help with heavy bleeding, cramps, endometriosis, acne, or mood issues do better on a 28-day cycle with 7 days of placebos.

Fact:

Traditional cyclic birth control can improve these symptoms in some folks, but others need to skip the placebo week to fully treat these symptoms, especially those who experience mood disturbances, painful periods and/or heavy bleeding.

Myth:

You need to menstruate regularly for your health.

Fact:

Regular menstruation is a sign of good health, not the cause of good health. If you’re missing your period because you’re skipping the placebos of your hormonal contraceptive, that’s no cause for concern. If you’re missing your period and you don’t know why, that may be a sign of a health issue that needs to get checked out by a doctor.

Myth:

Menstruating every month is more natural.

Fact:

Depends on what you mean by “natural.”

Having a period every month for years on end is a new phenomenon—for thousands of years of history, people who menstruated did so way less often, because they spent many more months of their lives either pregnant or breastfeeding.78
